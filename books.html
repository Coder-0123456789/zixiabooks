我的书

  1. 魔法拼音国

    从前，有一个神奇的拼音国。那里有声母城、韵母市和声调村，每一个字母都是一个可爱的小人儿。

    有一天，声母和韵母相遇了。它们通过拼合，帮助泡泡女孩实现各种愿望。故事同时在两个世界展开：一个是字母们的世界，一个是小女孩的世界。两个世界相互交织，让孩子在阅读故事的同时，自然而然地理解拼音的拼读规则。

    全套书共7册，通过生动有趣的故事，引出汉语拼音的全部声母和韵母。正文和游戏部分共涉及300多个音节，并结合孩子们熟悉的动物、植物和日常生活场景。

    在每册故事之后，还附有“r的日记”，用图文结合的方式总结拼音学习中的难点和标调规则。

    此外，配套的3册游戏手册延续了女孩和王子的探险故事，通过各种有趣的游戏帮助孩子巩固拼音知识。

    书中还附赠拼音卡片，可以通过多种互动方式进行学习和游戏。

    孩子们读完这套书，往往在不知不觉中掌握了完整的汉语拼音体系。

    购买此书：Amazon, DangDang

  2. 魔法树汉字启蒙翻翻书

    识字是阅读的基础，也是语文学习的重要起点。如何帮助儿童科学、有效地学习汉字，一直是家长和教育界广泛关注的问题。

    许多现有的汉字启蒙材料在选字方面缺乏系统性，数量和难度参差不齐；有些材料虽然设计成图书，却缺乏故事性，使得识字与阅读彼此分离。结果往往是：孩子认识了很多字，却很难真正阅读一本书。

    “魔法树”系列共4册翻翻书，精选了儿童汉字启蒙阶段最需要掌握的 216个基础汉字。大部分汉字都通过图形与图画进行形象化呈现，帮助孩子建立直观的理解。

    全书通过四个连续的小故事将这些汉字串联起来，让孩子在阅读故事的过程中自然识字，并通过阅读不断强化对汉字的理解。

    这套书适合 3—8岁 正在开始学习汉字的儿童，可以由家长朗读，也可以进行亲子共读，是一套兼具趣味性和系统性的汉字启蒙读物。

    购买此书: JingDong

  3. “小小语言家”汉语分级读物

    “分级阅读”最早在对外汉语教学领域得到广泛应用，例如“中文小书架”等项目就是较早的探索。而在母语儿童阅读领域，分级阅读的发展相对较晚。

    我曾就这一问题进行了大量调研，并撰写了《基础教育阶段应实施分级阅读》一文，倡议在基础教育阶段推广分级阅读。该文章发表于教育部的《教育决策咨询》内参。

    除了理论倡议，我也希望通过实践探索适合儿童的汉语分级读物。同时，这也是为了满足我女儿早期汉语阅读的需要。

    在创作过程中，我给自己设定了三个原则：

    • 科学分级

    • 故事有趣

    • 融入中国文化

    “小小语言家”系列共分为五个阅读阶段：

    1. 给我读 —— 父母为孩子朗读的亲子阅读

    2. 教我读 —— 父母为主、孩子参与的分享阅读

    3. 帮我读 —— 孩子为主、父母辅助的阅读

    4. 听我读 —— 孩子基本能够独立阅读

    5. 自己读 —— 完全自主阅读

    在有限的字词范围内写故事并不难，但要写出真正有趣、让孩子喜欢的故事却非常具有挑战。我乐于接受这样的挑战，也会继续以为自己孩子写书的初心，以及学术研究的严谨态度，持续创作更好的儿童读物。

My books

  1. Magic Pinyin Land

    Once upon a time, there was a magical land of pinyin. In this world there were Initial City, Final Town, and Tone Village, and every letter was a lively little character.

    One day, initials and finals met each other. By combining into syllables, they helped a girl named Bubble make her wishes come true. The story unfolds along two parallel lines: the world of letters and the world of the girl. As the two storylines intertwine, children naturally learn how pinyin syllables are formed.

    The complete series contains ten books, introducing all Mandarin initials and finals through engaging stories. More than 300 syllables appear throughout the stories and games, often connected to familiar themes such as animals, plants, and everyday objects.

    Each volume also includes “R’s Diary,” a visual summary that explains challenging points and tone-marking rules in an accessible way.

    In addition, three companion game books continue the adventure of the girl and the prince, helping children reinforce their learning through playful activities.

    The set also includes pinyin cards that can be used in many interactive games.

    By the time children finish the series, they often find that they have mastered the entire pinyin system almost effortlessly.

  2. The Magic Tree: My first Chinese Character Lift-the-Flap Books

    Learning characters is the foundation of reading and a crucial starting point for Chinese literacy. Helping children learn characters in a systematic and engaging way has long been a key concern for both parents and educators.

    Many existing early Chinese character books select characters rather randomly, with inconsistent difficulty levels. Others present characters in isolation without meaningful stories, separating character recognition from real reading. As a result, children may recognize many characters but still struggle to read a book independently.

    The Magic Tree series, a set of four lift-the-flap books, introduces 216 essential Chinese characters that are most useful for beginning learners.

    Most characters are visually connected with illustrations, helping children understand them through imagery. These characters are woven into four connected stories, allowing children to recognize characters through reading and reinforce them through repeated exposure in context.

    Designed for children aged 3–8, the books can be read aloud by parents or enjoyed together as shared reading. The series provides a playful yet systematic introduction to Chinese characters.

  3. The Little Linguist: Chinese Graded Readers

    The concept of graded reading was first widely developed in the field of teaching Chinese as a second language. Early projects such as Chinese Breeze explored this approach for language learners. In contrast, graded reading for native Chinese-speaking children has developed more slowly.

    To address this issue, I conducted extensive research and wrote the article “Graded Reading Should Be Implemented in Basic Education.” The article was published in the Ministry of Education’s policy journal Education Decision Consultation.

    Beyond advocating the idea, I also wanted to explore practical solutions. Part of the motivation came from a personal need: finding appropriate early reading materials for my own daughter.

    When writing the series, I set three guiding principles:

    • scientifically structured levels

    • engaging and enjoyable stories

    • integration of Chinese culture

    The Little Linguist series is organized into five reading stages:

    1. Read to Me — parent read-aloud

    2. Read with Me — shared reading led by parents

    3. Help Me Read — child-led reading with support

    4. Listen to Me Read — mostly independent reading

    5. I Can Read — fully independent reading

    Writing stories with limited vocabulary is not difficult, but creating stories that are truly engaging for children is much more challenging. I enjoy this challenge and will continue writing children’s books with both a parent’s heart and a researcher’s rigor.
